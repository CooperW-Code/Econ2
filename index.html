The Homeless Man and the Businessman Have More in Common Than You Think

By The Ledger  |  June 11, 2026  |  5 min read
Schwadron, Harley. We Have a Lot in Common. Cartoon Collections, 2024. CS440881.

There is a cartoon going around that has a homeless man sitting on the sidewalk with his hat out and speaking to a passing businessman in a suit. "We have a lot in common," the homeless man said, "neither of us pays income tax." It's funny. It's also, when you stop to think about it, a little uncomfortable. Because it points both ways. It's not just taking a jab at the person with nothing. It is also pointing the finger back at the man in the briefcase and begging the federal government to answer a question that has stubbornly remained unanswered for far too long: Why do the wealthiest individuals and companies in Canada pay so little taxes?

The answer is that they are not breaking the law, which is exactly why this all is so complex. Canada's tax code has been slowly accumulated over the past several decades and it's been augmented with so many tax credits, tax deferrals, and offshore loopholes that a wealthy individual or company is capable of navigating it all down to a tiny amount. An analysis from the Canadian Centre for Policy Alternatives shows that the wealthiest one percent in Canada paid an effective tax rate on their income of just 23.6 percent in 2022, as compared with 36.7 percent for the average worker. This means the richest Canadians pay less toward their tax bill as a portion of their overall earnings than the Canadians who make a fraction of that.

"The richest Canadians are handing over a smaller share of their income than people earning a fraction of what they make."

Capital gains also account for some of the problem. Prior to mid-2024, half of investment income was tax-exempt to individuals, meaning a CEO pulling most of their income through investment revenue was only taxed on half while a worker on a salary saw 100 percent of their income in the tax system. According to Canadians for Tax Fairness, the top 1.5 percent of earners received more than 61 percent of all capital gains. Over 60 percent of the associated tax break went to Canadians earning more than $250,000 per year. A slight increase in the inclusion rate was seen in the 2024 budget but this is not enough.

Offshore wealth is also a significant problem. It is a huge and largely unknown sum. Canadian corporations reported that in 2024 they had at least $682 billion in offshore assets. TSX 60 corporations alone had moved money around to take advantage of low-tax jurisdictions like Barbados and Luxembourg and therefore paid an estimated $7 billion less in taxes. According to the Tax Justice Network, corporate tax abuse and offshore concealment costs Canada an estimated $15 billion every year, all of which is lost revenue that was not invested or consumed in Canada.

It is understood that closing tax loopholes will likely have opponents who argue that investment will move out of the country and that the corporations are key for job creation. This has been the argument against raising taxes for three decades, even while the disparity between what corporations say to shareholders and what they owe the government in taxes continues to increase. A minimum book tax on large corporations, like the one passed by the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act, would ensure that corporations cannot rely on deferrals and deductions to bring their effective rate down to zero, just as the CCPA has proposed for Canada.

The cartoon is compelling because it's true. Both the poor man on the sidewalk and the man in the expensive suit are outside of the system of paying income taxes; one, however, has no other choice. The other has deliberately removed himself with the help of an accountant and an overseas registered holding company. The government should not treat these two people the same way. They should close the loopholes and create a baseline rate that the wealthiest corporations will owe on their profits. There is money there; it is just not being collected.

I get the frustration, but this piece glosses over some real risks. Canada's combined federal and provincial corporate tax rate sits at around 26.5%, which is not low compared to most OECD (Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development) member countries. If we close every deduction and pile on more taxes, we might just push investment and head offices south of the border, which would hurt workers more than shareholders. The cartoon is funny, but the situation is more complicated than "raise taxes and close loopholes." Some of those deductions exist because they incentivize R&D and job creation. Removing them without a careful plan could end up costing Canada more than it gains.
